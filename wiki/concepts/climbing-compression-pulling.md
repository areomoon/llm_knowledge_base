# Climbing Compression Pulling

> **TL;DR**: Compression climbing is pulling two holds *toward each other* rather
> than down — bicep, forearm-supinator, and pec-dominant, and a specific
> weakness most climbers never train because pull-ups train the wrong pattern.

## Definition

**Compression** is the climbing movement pattern where the two arms pull
*inward* (toward the body's midline) instead of *downward*. The force vector
is horizontal / diagonal rather than vertical. It shows up on:

- **Steep boards** (Moonboard 40°+) — especially close-spaced holds
- **Pinches** — inherently require squeezing inward
- **Sloper duals** — two slopers requiring opposing pull
- **Arête climbing** — body pressed into a corner

The failure pattern flagged in
[session-notes 2026-04-15](../../raw/climbing/session-2026-04-15/session-notes.md)
— "二頭內拉動作力量嚴重不足" — is a compression-pulling deficit, distinct from
vertical pull strength.

## How It Works

### Muscular recruitment differs from pull-ups

| Pattern | Dominant muscles | Joint action |
|---------|-----------------|--------------|
| Pull-up (vertical) | Lats, lower traps, biceps (secondary) | Shoulder extension + elbow flexion |
| Compression (horizontal) | Biceps, brachialis, pecs, serratus, forearm supinators | Shoulder adduction + elbow flexion + forearm supination |

A climber with strong pull-ups (e.g. 20 reps) can still be weak at compression
because **pull-ups don't train the adductor/bicep-supinated path**. This is
why the areomoon profile (20 pull-up baseline) still fails on
`LAST CALL`-style all-left Moonboard problems where compression onto close
holds is the crux.

### Training protocols (authority-cited)

| Drill | Protocol | Source |
|-------|----------|--------|
| Wide-grip ring rows | Pull rings apart while rowing, 3×10 | Hooper's Beta — *How to Train for Climbing [Full Body]* |
| Supinated chin-ups (close grip) | Narrow grip, palms facing you, 3×6–8 slow | Magnus Midtbø home training |
| Pinch block holds (heavy) | 2-hand pinch block, 3×20s, progressively heavier | Lattice Training — *How To Train For Slopers And Pinches* |
| Dumbbell pullover | Lying, DB behind head, squeeze in → builds serratus + lat-pec integration | Hooper's Beta antagonist series |
| Campus compression sims | Two close holds, body-tension holds at 90° — board-specific | Adam Ondra — *Moonboard training* clips |
| Dual sloper hangs | Two slopers, compress toward each other, 3×10s | Lattice Training — *Incredible Sloper Strength with the Heavy Roller* |
| Dumbbell curl supinated | 3×8–10 per arm, slow eccentric | General strength; also supports bicep tendon health per Hooper's Beta |

## Key Properties

- **Under-trained by default**: Standard pull and hangboard routines miss this
  entirely. Climbers discover the weakness only on specific terrain.
- **Bicep tendon vulnerability**: Aggressive compression on steep terrain is
  a top cause of bicep tendinopathy (Hooper's Beta — *Finger Flexor
  Tenosynovitis* series generalizes the mechanism). Warm up properly; ramp
  load slowly.
- **Board-specific transfer**: Moonboard 40° heavily rewards compression
  strength. Training on the board itself is among the most efficient PE-builders
  for this attribute (*Moonboard Vs Kilterboard* — ZP Climbs).
- **Grip overlap**: Compression often coincides with pinches/slopers, so
  training grip breadth (pinch block + sloper hangs) doubles the return.

## When to Use This Training

- Fail on steep-board problems (Moonboard 30°+, Kilterboard 40°+) where single
  moves work but linked compression fails.
- Notice "pulling in" moves (especially off close left or right holds) are
  weaker than "pulling down" moves.
- Pinches / slopers are a disproportionate weakness vs. crimps.

**Not for**: Climbers with active bicep/elbow tendinopathy — rehab first.
Compression work aggressively loads those tendons.
