# Climbing Training System

> **TL;DR**: A periodized training framework for climbing performance, with finger strength as the #1 limiting factor for grade progression, systematized by Lattice Training and validated by elite athletes like Adam Ondra and Alex Megos.

## Definition

Climbing training is the structured application of sport-science principles — periodization, progressive overload, specificity, and recovery — to rock climbing performance. Unlike general fitness, climbing training targets the unique demands of finger flexor tendons, contact strength, body tension, and movement technique simultaneously. The field is relatively young; most structured methodology emerged post-2010, largely through channels like **Lattice Training** (founded by Tom Randall and Ollie Torr) who pioneered data-driven assessment.

## How It Works

### Session Structure (Periodization Within a Session)

A well-designed climbing session follows this order:

1. **Warm-up** — dynamic movement, finger-specific warm-up, light hangboard
   - Reference: *Tomoa Narasaki Warming Up Routine* (TAMY Climbing), *Dynamic Climbing Warm-up* (The Climbing Doctor), *Finger Warm Up for Climbers* (Andrew MacFarlane)
2. **Skill/technique work** — footwork drills, movement sequences on easier terrain
   - Reference: *Intermediate Climbing Footwork Technique* (Lattice Training)
3. **Strength work** — hangboard, campus board, max-effort bouldering
   - Reference: *How to Train Finger Strength [Block Pulls Guide]* (Hooper's Beta), *How To Hangboard: Small Edge Finger Strength Training* (Lattice Training)
4. **Power endurance / volume** — longer circuits, board climbing, route laps
   - Reference: *Training Climbing Power Endurance - 3 Mistakes* (Lattice Training)
5. **Antagonist training & cool-down** — shoulder external rotation, wrist extension, stretching
   - Reference: *How to get Stronger with Antagonist Training* (Lattice Training)

### Macrocycle Design (Periodization Across Weeks)

Lattice Training's framework divides training into phases:
- **Assessment** — benchmark finger strength, pull-up max, body weight ratio
- **Base strength** — 4–8 weeks of hangboard, weighted pull-ups, core
- **Power/specificity** — board climbing (Moonboard/Kilterboard), limit bouldering
- **Performance** — projecting, reducing training volume
- **Rest/deload** — mandatory recovery weeks

Key video: *How to Write Your Own Training Plan* (Lattice Training), *Detailed Training Plan for Rock Climbing* (Lattice Training), *Most Climbers Should Train LIKE THIS* (Adam Ondra).

## Key Properties

- **Finger strength is the #1 limiter**: For most climbers below V10/7c+, max finger strength (measured via hangboard) correlates most strongly with grade ceiling. Alex Megos and Emil Abrahamsson both confirm this — *How to get STRONG FINGERS with Alex Megos*, *How climbers get STRONG fingers (3 methods)*.
- **Specificity matters**: Training method must match the target demand — sloper strength requires different training than crimp strength. See *Incredible Sloper Strength with the Heavy Roller* (Lattice Training), *How To Train For Slopers And Pinches* (EpicTV).
- **Antagonist work prevents injury**: Imbalanced training (pulling without pushing) causes shoulder impingement and elbow tendinopathy. *How to get Stronger with Antagonist Training* (Lattice Training).
- **Body weight vs. strength ratio**: *Climbing: Stay Light or Get Strong?* (Lattice Training) — the optimal answer is athlete-specific and depends on current strength levels.
- **Intermediate plateau is common**: Most climbers stagnate between V4–V7 due to over-climbing and under-training. *How To Avoid The 'Intermediate Climber' Plateau* (Lattice Training).

## Finger Strength Training Methods

Three main approaches (per Emil Abrahamsson — *How climbers get STRONG fingers (3 methods)*):

1. **Max Hangs** — 10–12 seconds at 85–95% max load, full rest between sets. Best for max strength.
2. **Repeaters** — 7 seconds on / 3 seconds off × 6 reps per set. Better for endurance adaptation.
3. **Block Pulls / Dead Hangs on edges** — controlled, measurable, low injury risk for beginners.

Common beginner mistakes (per *V15 Climber on Most Common Hangboarding Mistakes*, The Nugget Climbing):
- Starting too heavy, too soon
- Insufficient rest between sessions (tendons need 48–72h recovery)
- Using crimp grip exclusively (half-crimp safer long-term)

## Injury Prevention

Climbing injuries concentrate in the fingers (A2 pulley tears, tenosynovitis) and shoulders. Hooper's Beta (medical background) provides the most authoritative public guidance:

| Injury | Video | Key Insight |
|--------|-------|-------------|
| Finger tenosynovitis | *Finger Flexor Tenosynovitis in Rock Climbers* | Inflammation of tendon sheath; requires rest + gradual reload |
| Swollen finger joints | *Why Are My Finger Joints Swollen* | Often synovitis; distinguish from pulley injury |
| Wrist pain | *Fix Your Wrist Pain! Follow-Along Routine* (Tone and Tighten), *Basic Wrist Stability Training for Climbers* (Hooper's Beta) | Wrist extensors/flexors need balanced training |
| General injury | *Inaccurate Climbing Advice I Hear Every Day* (Hooper's Beta) | Critiques commonly repeated myths |

**Prevention rule**: Never train through sharp finger pain. Tendon damage accumulates silently and ruptures suddenly.

## Training Plan Design Principles

From *How to Write Your Own Training Plan* (Lattice Training) and *How to Plan Your Climbing Training: Troubleshooting* (Lattice Training):

1. **Identify your limiter** — finger strength, power endurance, technique, or body tension?
2. **Set a training frequency** — 3–4 days/week climbing, 1–2 days supplemental strength
3. **Progressive overload** — increase load by ~5% when you can complete all sets/reps cleanly
4. **Deload every 4–6 weeks** — reduce volume by 40–50%, maintain intensity
5. **Track everything** — hang weight, sets, grades attempted, how fingers felt

Adam Ondra's #1 rule (*The Nugget* interview): **Rest more than you think you need.** Elite athletes chronically under-recover.

## When to Use

- **Beginner (V0–V4)**: Focus on technique, footwork, and volume. Minimal structured hangboard. Risk of tendon injury from premature loading.
- **Intermediate (V4–V7)**: Primary plateau zone. Begin structured hangboard (2×/week), add antagonist work, start tracking metrics.
- **Advanced (V7–V10)**: Individualized periodization, limit bouldering on training boards, assess specific weaknesses with Lattice-style benchmarks.
- **Not applicable**: Climbers with active finger injuries — rehab protocol first.
