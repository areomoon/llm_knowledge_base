# Climbing Lock-Off Strength

> **TL;DR**: Lock-off strength is the ability to hold an arm in a statically flexed
> position (elbow 90°–fully closed) while the opposite hand reaches — the single
> biggest determinant of whether a climber can make controlled, static moves
> between holds rather than dynoing, and a near-universal weakness on one side
> versus the other.

## Definition

A **lock-off** is an isometric hold in which the working arm is bent (typically
at 90°, 120°, or "deep" / fully closed) supporting body weight, while the free
hand reaches to the next hold. In climbing literature this is also called an
"L-position" when one arm is bent high and the body hangs sideways.

Lock-off strength is distinct from:
- **Max pull strength** (one-rep pull-up) — concentric, dynamic
- **Contact strength** (finger/grip) — forearm-dominant
- **Power endurance** — repeated effort

It is fundamentally **isometric**, at a specific joint angle, with a large
contribution from the biceps, brachialis, and scapular retractors (*EpicTV —
Mastering Lock Offs When Climbing*).

## How It Works

The limiter is torque production at the bent elbow while the scapula stays
depressed and retracted. Two mechanisms fail most often:

1. **Strength-at-angle gap**: Climbers strong at full extension (hang) and full
   flexion (pull-up top) are weak at mid-range 90°–120° — the exact angle needed
   to reach.
2. **Unilateral asymmetry**: Most climbers have a dominant-side preference. The
   weaker side fails first on moves requiring that specific lock. Lattice
   Training flags this as a frequent cause of plateau (*How To Avoid The
   'Intermediate Climber' Plateau*).

### Training protocols (authority-cited)

| Drill | Protocol | Source |
|-------|----------|--------|
| Weighted pull-up with pause | Pause 3–5s at 90° elbow on the way up AND down | *Mastering Lock Offs When Climbing* (EpicTV) |
| Typewriter / L-shift | From a full pull-up, shift side-to-side slowly | Magnus Midtbø — home training videos |
| One-arm lock-off hold | Assisted (towel/band on weak arm), hold 5–10s × 3–5 sets, both sides | Lattice Training — *One Arm Pull Up Training* framework |
| Offset pull-ups | One hand on bar, other on a towel ~30cm lower → builds single-side lock strength | Emil Abrahamsson — home training series |
| Frenchies | Pull-up → pause 5s at top → pause 5s at 90° → pause 5s at 120° → down. 3–5 reps. | Adam Ondra — *Most Climbers Should Train LIKE THIS* |

## Key Properties

- **Angle-specific**: Train the angle you need. A climber who can lock deep but
  not at 90° will still fail mid-reach (Lattice Training).
- **Symmetry required**: Always train the weak side to at least equal volume,
  often more. Left-right asymmetry >15% predicts injury risk (generic sports
  science; Hooper's Beta cites this in shoulder-balance discussions).
- **Recovery is fast**: Unlike fingers (48–72h), lock-off work at moderate load
  can be done 2–3×/week safely.
- **Specificity beats volume**: Climbing-specific drills (campus half-reps,
  on-wall lock reaches) transfer better than pure pull-up variants once a
  strength base exists (Adam Ondra).

## When to Use This Training

- Climber can pull-up 15+ reps but fails to reach holds statically → pure
  lock-off limiter, prioritize this.
- Detectable left/right asymmetry (one side blows out first on mirror moves) →
  unilateral programming is mandatory; ignoring it calcifies the imbalance.
- Grade plateau at V5–V7 on vertical/slight-overhang with static crux moves.

**Not for**: Climbers still building base pull strength (<8 pull-ups), or
climbers whose limiter is finger strength (see
[Climbing Training System](climbing-training-system.md)). Fix the base first.
