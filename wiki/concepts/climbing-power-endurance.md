# Climbing Power Endurance

> **TL;DR**: Power endurance (PE) is the ability to repeat near-maximal
> contractions under accumulating forearm fatigue — the reason a climber who can
> do every move on a V7 linked together still falls at the crux. Trained through
> structured lactic-tolerance protocols, not by "climbing more."

## Definition

Power endurance is the capacity to sustain **high-intensity efforts** (≥70% of
max contact strength) across a **continuous sequence** lasting ~20 seconds to
~2 minutes, in the presence of rapidly accumulating fatigue (hydrogen ion
buildup and forearm occlusion).

It sits between:
- **Max strength / power** — a single move at limit effort (<10s)
- **Aerobic endurance** — low-intensity climbing for >5 min (easy routes, volume)

The hallmark diagnostic: *single moves and broken sections work, but linking
them end-to-end fails* — the exact pattern flagged in
[session-notes 2026-04-15](../../raw/climbing/session-2026-04-15/session-notes.md).

## How It Works

### Physiology

PE failure is forearm-flexor occlusion + lactate buffering limits. When a
climber grips a hold at >50% max, blood flow to the forearm is partially cut
off. Hydrogen ion (H+) accumulates, interfering with muscle contraction. The
"pump" is this state. Training PE adapts:

- Capillary density — more blood flow into the muscle
- Mitochondrial density — better clearance of lactate
- Neural tolerance — ability to push through the burn signal

### Training protocols (Lattice Training — *Training Climbing Power Endurance - 3 Mistakes*)

The three common mistakes:
1. **Intensity too low** — "4x4s" on flash-grade problems doesn't stress PE; it
   trains aerobic capacity.
2. **Rest too long** — PE adaptation requires incomplete recovery between efforts.
3. **Volume without structure** — endlessly climbing easy routes improves capacity
   marginally compared to structured intervals.

### Protocols

| Method | Structure | When |
|--------|-----------|------|
| 4×4 bouldering | 4 problems × 4 sends back-to-back, rest equal to work | PE base |
| Linked circuits | 30–60 moves continuous at ~2 grades below limit, rest 1:1 | mid-cycle |
| On-the-minute (OTM) | 1 send per minute for 6–10 min on your flash grade | peaking |
| Route laps | 3 routes back-to-back, rest 2 min | sport-route-specific |
| Repeaters on board | 20–30 moves × 3–4 sets, 3 min rest | advanced |

Adam Ondra's principle (*Most Climbers Should Train LIKE THIS*): PE training
must be **specific to the target style**. Vertical-route PE uses different
protocols than steep-bouldering link-ups.

## Key Properties

- **Intensity governs specificity**: PE at 60% max builds one adaptation; PE at
  85% max builds another. Train both across a cycle.
- **Crosses with finger strength**: A climber with weak fingers will pump out
  early regardless of "endurance" training — PE training assumes sufficient
  max strength. Lattice: "you can't endure what you can't do."
- **Tapers quickly**: PE adaptation peaks after 3–4 weeks then stalls. Cycle
  it — don't train year-round.
- **Don't confuse with aerobic capacity**: Easy-route volume builds capillary
  base; it's a prerequisite, not a substitute.

## When to Use This Training

- Can execute every move on project grade individually, but fails the link-up
  → textbook PE limiter.
- Last ¼ of a boulder/route is where you fall despite moves being "easy"
  relative to the crux.
- Target style is sustained (20+ moves) rather than short/powerful (<10 moves).

**Not for**: Climbers who fail at the crux move itself (max strength limiter),
or beginners whose technique is the actual gap. Train the right thing.
