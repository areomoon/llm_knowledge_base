# China Tech 35-Age Crisis

> **TL;DR**: Chinese big-tech companies are structurally designed to extract maximum output from engineers in their 20s–early 30s and cheaply offload them after 35 via deliberate deskilling, high attrition pressure, and soft layoffs — a system the author calls "low buy, high sell" applied to human capital.

## Definition

The "35-age crisis" (35歲危機) describes a systemic phenomenon in Chinese internet big-tech (BAT, ByteDance, Meituan, etc.) where engineers and technical workers face near-certain involuntary exit between ages 33–38. This is not an individual failure — it is the designed equilibrium outcome of how these firms structure talent pipelines: recruit ambitious graduates, extract peak output under high pressure, then shed expensive senior staff before they become either too costly or too entrenched.

The mechanism is self-reinforcing: companies simultaneously *create* the crisis (through spiral-down pressures) and *use* it to justify replacement with cheaper younger cohorts.

## How It Works

### The Employer Logic

Big-tech firms prefer younger workers because they are:
- Cheaper (entry salary vs. senior salary gap can be 3–5×)
- Higher energy, fewer family constraints
- Less likely to push back on scope or hours
- Not yet "institutionalized" — willing to attempt things experienced engineers consider impossible

The company's active countermeasure against senior employee leverage: **deliberate螺絲釘化** (screw-ization) — fragmenting each role to the smallest possible scope so no individual becomes indispensable. This destroys transferable skill depth.

### The Four Attrition Levers (Soft Layoff Toolkit)

Companies rarely formally lay off. Instead, they create conditions that cause voluntary exit:

1. **Sustained high-pressure environment** — 996 schedules erode physical capacity over time; the body gives out before HR acts
2. **Extreme commute burden** — especially after employees form families; 2–3 hour daily commutes at 996 intensity become untenable
3. **Political trap exposure** — one mistake in a large bureaucracy blocks promotion permanently; trapped employees eventually leave
4. **Dependency on specific managers** — when a supportive manager exits, their reports lose political cover and often follow
5. **Product-line uncertainty** — employees on declining product lines lose growth trajectory and self-select out

### The Advancement Distribution (<10% vs 90%)

**Technical track (Architect, Principal, Fellow):**
- Income ceiling: 700k–1.5M RMB; top 200k RMB
- Post-departure income drops 60–80% — skills are company-specific, not market-transferable
- Reality: senior technologists spend most time in alignment meetings, not coding
- Heavy luck dependency: requires backing the right leadership chain and the right product line

**Management track:** even harder — oral communication and political capital weigh more than technical depth; managers stranded in resource pools eventually self-exit.

**The 90%:** exit via the soft-layoff toolkit above, framed internally as "优化" (optimization) or "delivering talent to society."

## Post-35 Destination Distribution

| Destination | ~Share | Mechanism | Key Constraint |
|-------------|--------|-----------|----------------|
| Small/medium company, technical role | ~1/3 | Big-tech brand still valuable; becomes tech lead | Halo degrades after 2–3 company hops; income ceiling hit |
| Training institution / vocational college | 10–20% | Lecture job using big-tech credentials | Oral skill matters more than code skill |
| State-owned enterprise / civil service | ~1/3 | Stability over income; often becomes former employer's client | Large income drop; permanent lifestyle downgrade |
| Remote freelance / offshore contracts | <10% | 3–6× China/US salary arbitrage; relationship-gated | Requires existing network; no job boards; health risk (sedentary) |
| Successful entrepreneur | <5% | Supplier to former employer, short-video, SaaS | Pure survivorship bias |

### Counter-Intuitive Finding: Big-Tech Skills Are Weaker Than Expected

The article notes that big-tech engineers often have *weaker* general technical skills than small-company peers, for three reasons:
1. Closed proprietary stacks (internal tools not used anywhere else)
2. Deliberate role narrowing — each person owns a tiny slice
3. 996 leaves no bandwidth for learning outside the current task

High-level skills (system design, architecture) can only be developed through project exposure, not study — and big-tech's螺絲釘化 explicitly blocks this.

## Key Properties

- **Structural, not cyclical**: the 35-age crisis is not a downturn artifact — it persists through boom and bust because it serves the firms' permanent economic interests
- **Soft exit, not layoff**: the mechanism is environmental pressure, not headcount reduction — this makes it harder to prepare against and easier for firms to deny
- **Transferability penalty**: the more a company employs internal tools and螺絲釘化, the lower the market value of that employee's accumulated experience
- **Family-triggered acceleration**: the crisis intensifies precisely when life costs peak (mortgage, childcare, elder care) because family formation degrades commute flexibility and risk tolerance

## The Author's Five Principles (跑哥)

1. **The window is shorter than you think** — start building alternative income sources while energy is still high
2. **Networks are for presence, not extraction** — maintain weak ties passively; be findable when needed rather than transactional
3. **Emotional intelligence compounds** — listen more, argue less; political missteps are permanent at scale
4. **Accept ordinary** — most people who excelled in gaokao cannot accept being average; this ego trap drives reckless late-career gambles that lose life savings
5. **Health is the real capital** — physical breakdown is the terminal event; it cannot be reversed by salary or status

## Applicability Outside China

The phenomenon is China-specific in intensity but not in kind. Structural equivalents exist globally:

| Factor | China Big-Tech | SG/US Big-Tech |
|--------|---------------|----------------|
| Deliberate螺絲釘化 | Severe | Moderate |
| Age discrimination after 35 | Systemic, near-universal | Present but less structured; varies by firm |
| Soft layoff toolkit | Highly developed | Exists but weaker (stronger labor law) |
| Skill transferability penalty | High | Moderate (more open-source, public cloud) |
| Timeline to forced exit | 8–12 years post-grad | Less deterministic |

**Estimated SG applicability: 30–50%** — the structural risks are present at lower intensity.
