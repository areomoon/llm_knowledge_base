# Technical-to-Business Transition

> **TL;DR**: Technical IC → VC/Strategy is a 6-year gradual expansion, not a career switch — tech credibility is the unfair advantage that makes a business person rare.

## Definition

Technical-to-Business Transition is a career framework for deep-tech practitioners who want to move toward business strategy, VC, or corporate development. Rather than abandoning technical work, the practitioner uses technical depth as an entry credential while progressively accumulating business skills and network, until the two converge into a role where both are required simultaneously.

The core asymmetry: business people who "understand tech" are common; engineers who can make sound commercial decisions are scarce. VC firms, corporate strategy teams, and BD orgs pay a premium for the latter.

## How It Works

The transition follows four stages, each building on the previous:

1. **Technical IC (Year 0–2)**: Build domain depth and production credibility. Begin writing business-angle analysis content in parallel. Join local startup/VC ecosystem events.

2. **Technical Lead / PM (Year 2–4)**: Move into roles that require both technical judgment and stakeholder communication. Accumulate deal memos and commercial exposure. Build network actively.

3. **Technical Strategy / BD (Year 4–6)**: Transition into roles where the primary output is commercial decisions informed by technical diligence — BD, venture partner, technical due diligence, solutions consulting.

4. **VC Partner / Strategy Director / Founder (Year 6+)**: Full business-side role where technical background is a durable moat, not a job description.

## Key Properties

- **Non-binary**: No forced choice between "stay technical" or "go business." The path merges both.
- **Credibility-gated**: Skipping the technical IC phase removes the only differentiator from pure MBA candidates. Shortcuts undermine the strategy.
- **Content-accelerated**: Writing business analysis from a technical perspective compresses network-building by making the practitioner visible to VCs, founders, and BD leads who self-select as readers.
- **SG-favorable**: Singapore's zero capital gains tax, dense deep-tech VC ecosystem (SGInnovate, Wavemaker, Monk's Hill), and position as SEA's deal hub makes it an ideal geography for this transition.

## Deal Memo as Skill-Building Tool

A **deal memo** is a 2-page investment thesis document used by VC firms to evaluate startups. Writing one monthly — even without a VC affiliation — builds the analytical vocabulary, market-sizing instinct, and technology evaluation frameworks required for the target roles.

Structure of a practice deal memo:
- What does the company do? (1 paragraph, no jargon)
- How large is the market? (TAM/SAM/SOM)
- What is the technical moat? (can an engineer clone this in 6 months?)
- Who are the competitors? (why this team wins)
- Would I invest? (explicit yes/no with reasoning)

Publishing 12 deal memos on LinkedIn creates an inbound signal: VCs and founders in the practitioner's target ecosystem will initiate contact.

## Networking 3-Layer Model

| Layer | Mode | Timeline | Goal |
|-------|------|----------|------|
| L1: Online weak ties | LinkedIn articles, Twitter/X engagement | Start now | Visibility to VCs, founders, BD leads |
| L2: Local in-person | Monthly meetups (SGInnovate, NUS Enterprise, Startup Grind SG) | After joining employer | 3 business cards/event → LinkedIn connect next day |
| L3: Deep relationships | Coffee chats, recurring contact | 6 months after L2 start | 3–5 people who become transition bridges |

The 35-year-old Chinese tech worker analysis confirms: relationships are assets to cultivate continuously, not tools to deploy when needed. L3 relationships built before the transition need is felt are worth 10x those built during an active job search.

## Three Exit Paths

| Exit | Role | How to get there | Comp range |
|------|------|-----------------|------------|
| A — VC | Technical Due Diligence / Venture Partner | 2–3 yr at current employer → deal memo portfolio + SG VC network | $150–300k USD + carry |
| B — Corporate | Strategy / BD at AI company | 2–3 yr → jump to BD/solutions at AI-native firm | $200–400k USD |
| C — Founder | AI startup | Find unsolved problem at employer, build with technical + commercial understanding | $0 or $10M+ |

## When to Use

- When a technical practitioner has domain depth but wants long-term career direction in business/investment rather than engineering management.
- When choosing between deepening technical specialization vs. accumulating commercial exposure — the answer is "both, sequentially."
- When evaluating whether to take a PM, BD, or solutions role as a "step down" — frame it as stage 2 of a deliberate transition, not a demotion.

## Failure Modes

- **Too early transition**: Moving to BD/PM before establishing technical credibility removes the differentiator. Minimum 2 years of production-level technical work in the target domain.
- **Invisible transition**: Doing the work without creating visible artifacts (articles, deal memos, public talks). Technical people are systematically underexposed in business networks; content creation corrects this asymmetry.
- **Wrong self-presentation**: Introducing as "algorithm engineer" instead of "I use AI to solve X and Y problems" signals coder, not strategist. Language shapes which opportunities appear.
